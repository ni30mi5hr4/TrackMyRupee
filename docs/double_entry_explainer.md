# What is Double-Entry Bookkeeping? A Plain English Guide

No accounting degree needed. No jargon. Just a simple explanation of what double-entry bookkeeping is, why it exists, and how it works — using everyday examples.

---

## The Core Idea in One Sentence

> **Every time money moves, it comes FROM somewhere and goes TO somewhere.**

That's it. Double-entry just means you write down both sides of every transaction — where the money came from, and where it went.

---

## Why Does This Matter?

Imagine you have ₹10,000 in your wallet. You spend ₹500 on dinner.

Most people just write: *"Spent ₹500 on food."*

But that sentence is incomplete. It doesn't tell you:
- Did the money come from your wallet? Your bank account? A friend's loan?
- Is your wallet balance now ₹9,500? Or did you pay by card?

Double-entry forces you to answer both questions **every single time**. This makes your records complete, accurate, and impossible to accidentally fudge.

---

## The Golden Rule

Every transaction always has **two equal and opposite effects**:

| Effect 1 | Effect 2 |
|---|---|
| Something **increases** | Something else **decreases** |
| Money arrives somewhere | Money leaves somewhere else |

These two sides are traditionally called **Debit** and **Credit** — but ignore those words for now. Just think: **"in"** and **"out"**, or **"gained"** and **"lost"**.

---

## Real Life Examples

### Example 1: You Receive Your Salary

You get ₹50,000 salary deposited into your bank account.

| What happened | Which account | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Your bank balance went up | Bank Account | +₹50,000 |
| You earned income | Salary / Income | +₹50,000 |

Both sides are ₹50,000. They balance. ✅

---

### Example 2: You Pay Your Electricity Bill

You transfer ₹1,200 from your bank to pay the electricity bill.

| What happened | Which account | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Your bank balance went down | Bank Account | −₹1,200 |
| You paid an expense | Electricity Expense | +₹1,200 |

Total effect: ₹1,200 left your bank, ₹1,200 was recorded as an expense. They balance. ✅

---

### Example 3: You Transfer Money Between Accounts

You move ₹5,000 from your savings account to your current account.

| What happened | Which account | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Savings account went down | Savings Account | −₹5,000 |
| Current account went up | Current Account | +₹5,000 |

No money was created or lost — it just moved. Both sides balance. ✅

---

### Example 4: You Borrow Money From a Friend

A friend lends you ₹3,000 in cash.

| What happened | Which account | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Your cash went up | Cash / Wallet | +₹3,000 |
| You now owe someone | Loan Payable | +₹3,000 |

Your cash increased, but so did your debt. Net effect on your wealth: zero. Both sides balance. ✅

---

## Why One-Sided Records Break

Imagine you only wrote: *"Bank account decreased by ₹1,200"*.

A month later you look at your bank statement and think — where did that ₹1,200 go? Was it a bill? A transfer? A mistake? A fraud?

With double-entry, you always know, because the other side of the record tells you exactly what happened.

---

## The "Scale" Mental Model

Think of every transaction as a weighing scale. Both sides must always be equal.

```
        LEFT PAN              RIGHT PAN
    (where it went)        (where it came from)
    ─────────────────      ─────────────────
      Groceries ₹800    =    Bank Account ₹800
```

If you put something on one side, you must put the same weight on the other. The scale must always balance. If it doesn't balance, something was recorded wrong.

---

## What Are "Accounts"?

In double-entry, an **account** is just a named bucket that tracks one thing. Examples:

- **Bank Account** — tracks your bank balance
- **Wallet / Cash** — tracks physical cash
- **Salary** — tracks money you earned from work
- **Groceries** — tracks what you spent on food
- **Rent** — tracks what you spent on housing
- **Loan from Friend** — tracks what you owe someone

Every transaction touches exactly **two** of these buckets.

## Quick Recap

| Concept | Plain English |
|---|---|
| Double-entry | Writing down both sides of every transaction |
| Account | A named bucket tracking one thing (bank, cash, salary, groceries…) |
| Balanced entry | The two sides of a transaction are always equal |
| Why it's useful | You always know where money came from AND where it went |
| Where you see it | Banks, businesses, apps like TrackMyRupee — anywhere money is tracked seriously |

---

## One Final Analogy

Think of water flowing through pipes.

- Water doesn't appear from nowhere.
- Water doesn't disappear into nothing.
- When water leaves one pipe, it enters another.

Money works the same way. Double-entry is just the system that makes sure every drop of water (every rupee) is accounted for — both where it left and where it arrived.
